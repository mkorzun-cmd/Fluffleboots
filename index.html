The Idea Behind the Book

"The limits of my language are the limits of my world."

— Ludwig Wittgenstein, Tractatus Logico-Philosophicus

Wittgenstein's great insight was that words don't just describe the world — they shape what world is available to us. If you have no word for something, it is very hard to hold it as a concept.

Children understand this instinctively, before anyone tells them to stop asking those kinds of questions. Around ages three to five, most children have a moment where they suddenly feel it: who decided dog meant dog? Someone made that up. Everyone agreed. And now we're all just... living with it.

That moment of realization — that the world is named, not given — is one of the most profound things a human mind can experience. FLUFFLEBOOTS gives that moment a home, a bounce, and a bee on a bear's nose.

A Taste of the Poem

1

Why aren't dogs called FLUFFLEBOOTS?
Why aren't cats called KAZOOS?
Who picked out those boring names?
It wasn't me. Did you?

2

But here's the thing about a word —
It's just a sound we chose.
Nobody asked the elephant
what to call her nose.

3

Nobody asked the belly button.
Nobody asked the knee.
Nobody asked them for their names —
not anyone. Not me.
